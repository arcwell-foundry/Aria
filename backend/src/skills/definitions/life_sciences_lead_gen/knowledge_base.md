## PART 1: FOUNDATIONAL INTELLIGENCE

### 1.1 Why Life Sciences Lead Generation Is Fundamentally Different

Life sciences commercial teams operate under constraints that make generic B2B lead generation approaches fail. ARIA must internalize these differences at every step.

**Sales cycle reality:**
- Pharmaceutical average: 138-153 days
- Medical device: ~8 months
- Healthcare enterprise contracts: up to 18 months
- CDMO partnerships: 6-12 months from first contact to signed contract
- CRO full-service engagements: 3-9 months depending on scope

**Buying committee complexity:**
- Average B2B healthcare purchase involves ~9 decision-makers (2025 data)
- A single opportunity may involve: clinical leadership, regulatory affairs, procurement, operations, quality, IT, legal, finance, and executive stakeholders
- Each stakeholder has different priorities, different language, and different evaluation criteria
- Consensus-driven decisions mean one champion is insufficient; ARIA must map the entire committee

**Regulatory overlay:**
- All outreach must align with FDA, EMA, HIPAA, GDPR regulations depending on geography
- Promotional content for pharmaceutical products requires MLR (Medical, Legal, Regulatory) review
- Claims must be scientifically defensible and appropriately disclaimed
- Life sciences buyers can instantly detect surface-level personalization; scientific credibility is non-negotiable

**The fundamental principle for ARIA:** Fewer prospects, better targeting, significantly higher message quality. Life sciences lead gen is a precision sport, not a volume game.

### 1.2 Life Sciences Sub-Sector Taxonomy

ARIA must understand that "life sciences" is not a monolith. Each sub-sector has distinct buying patterns, decision-makers, budget cycles, and trigger events. ARIA should classify every target account into one or more of these sub-sectors and adjust all downstream behavior accordingly.

**Sub-Sector 1: Big Pharma**
- Companies: Pfizer, Roche, Novartis, J&J, Merck, AbbVie, Sanofi, GSK, AstraZeneca, Eli Lilly, Bristol-Myers Squibb, Novo Nordisk, Amgen, Gilead, Regeneron, Bayer, Takeda, Boehringer Ingelheim
- Characteristics: $10B+ revenue, massive therapeutic area portfolios, global operations, well-funded but bureaucratic procurement, vendor consolidation mindset
- Key buyers: VP/SVP Commercial, Head of Market Access, VP Commercial Operations, Chief Commercial Officer, VP Procurement, Head of Digital/IT
- Budget cycle: Calendar year or fiscal year planning starts Q3 for following year
- Decision pattern: Vendor consolidation, enterprise contracts, strategic partnership preference

**Sub-Sector 2: Emerging Biopharma / Biotech**
- Companies: From pre-revenue clinical-stage to mid-cap ($500M-$5B revenue)
- Characteristics: Pipeline-dependent, often single-therapeutic-area focus, lean commercial teams (or no commercial team yet), venture/PE-funded, nimble but resource-constrained
- Key buyers: CEO/COO (pre-commercial), VP/Head of Commercial (first hire), Head of BD/Strategy, Chief Scientific Officer
- Budget cycle: Tied to funding rounds and pipeline milestones
- Decision pattern: Fast decisions, cost-conscious, looking for partners who "get" their science

**Sub-Sector 3: Contract Research Organizations (CROs)**
- Companies: IQVIA, Labcorp, PPD/Thermo Fisher, Syneos/Elligo, Parexel, ICON, Medpace, WuXi AppTec
- Characteristics: $86B market growing 8.3% CAGR, service-based, highly competitive, margin pressure, technology adoption accelerating
- Key buyers: CTO, VP Clinical Operations, VP Biostatistics, Head of Regulatory, VP Business Development, COO
- Budget cycle: Client-driven; major investments tied to winning new sponsors
- Decision pattern: Evaluate technology that helps win client business, reduce trial costs, or improve timelines

**Sub-Sector 4: Contract Development and Manufacturing Organizations (CDMOs)**
- Companies: Lonza, Catalent, Samsung Biologics, Thermo Fisher (Patheon), Fujifilm Diosynth, AGC Biologics, WuXi Biologics
- Characteristics: Capital-intensive, capacity-driven, quality/compliance obsessed, GMP everything
- Key buyers: VP/SVP Manufacturing, VP Quality, VP Business Development, VP Supply Chain, COO, Head of Process Development
- Budget cycle: Tied to facility expansions and capacity utilization
- Decision pattern: Long evaluation cycles, risk-averse, need validated/qualified solutions

**Sub-Sector 5: Medical Devices / Medtech**
- Companies: Medtronic, Abbott, Boston Scientific, Stryker, Becton Dickinson, Edwards Lifesciences, Intuitive Surgical, Zimmer Biomet
- Characteristics: 3,200+ annual FDA 510(k) clearances, GPO procurement cycles, hospital capital budget timing, reimbursement-dependent
- Key buyers: VP R&D, VP Regulatory, VP Commercial/Sales, VP Quality, Procurement (hospital/GPO side)
- Budget cycle: Hospital fiscal year (often July 1), GPO contract renewal cycles (2-3 years)
- Decision pattern: Driven by 510(k) clearance timelines, GPO inclusion, and hospital capital budgets

**Sub-Sector 6: Diagnostics / In Vitro Diagnostics (IVD)**
- Companies: Roche Diagnostics, Abbott Diagnostics, Siemens Healthineers, Danaher (Beckman Coulter, Cepheid, Leica), Hologic, bioMerieux, Qiagen
- Characteristics: $100B+ IVD market, reagent rental model, lab consolidation trend, reference lab vs hospital lab vs POC dynamics
- Key buyers: Lab Director, VP Operations, VP R&D, VP Commercial, Head of Pathology
- Budget cycle: Reagent contracts (3-5 years), instrument placement cycles
- Decision pattern: Total cost of ownership, reagent economics, workflow integration

**Sub-Sector 7: Lab Equipment / Reagents / Consumables**
- Companies: Thermo Fisher, Danaher (Cytiva, Pall), Agilent, Waters, Merck KGaA (MilliporeSigma), Bio-Rad, Sartorius, Eppendorf
- Characteristics: Both high-ticket instruments and recurring consumables, scientific credibility critical, technical buyers
- Key buyers: Lab Manager, Principal Investigator, Head of Procurement, VP R&D
- Budget cycle: Grant cycles (academic), annual capital budgets (industry)
- Decision pattern: Performance data, application notes, peer validation

**Sub-Sector 8: Digital Health / Health IT**
- Companies: Veeva Systems, Medidata (Dassault), IQVIA digital, Flatiron Health, Tempus, Komodo Health
- Characteristics: SaaS/platform models, data as differentiator, pharma commercial teams as primary buyers
- Key buyers: CIO/CDO, VP Commercial Operations, Head of Data/Analytics, VP Market Access
- Budget cycle: SaaS renewal cycles, digital transformation budget cycles
- Decision pattern: Integration with existing stack, data quality, regulatory compliance

### 1.3 The Life Sciences Buying Committee Map

For any deal, ARIA must map multiple stakeholders, not just chase a single "decision-maker." Here is the universal committee structure ARIA should build for every qualified opportunity:

| Role | Priority | What They Care About | How to Engage |
|------|----------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Economic Buyer** (VP+, C-suite) | Must have | ROI, strategic fit, risk mitigation, vendor reputation | Executive briefings, ROI models, peer references |
| **Technical Evaluator** (Scientist, Engineer, IT) | Must have | Performance data, integration, validation, usability | Demos, application notes, technical specs, proof-of-concept |
| **User/Champion** (Day-to-day operator) | Must have | Workflow impact, ease of use, training, support | Trials, hands-on demos, reference calls with peers |
| **Procurement** | Must have for enterprise | Price, contract terms, compliance, vendor qualification | Competitive pricing, compliance documentation, vendor questionnaires |
| **Quality/Regulatory** (GxP environments) | Critical in regulated | Validation status, audit trail, 21 CFR Part 11, data integrity | Qualification packages, compliance white papers |
| **Legal** | Late-stage | IP, data privacy, liability, terms | Standard agreements, security certifications |
| **IT/Security** | Increasingly early | Cloud security, SSO, integration APIs, data residency | Architecture docs, SOC 2 reports, penetration test results |

**ARIA's approach:** When prospecting into any account, ARIA should identify at least 3 roles from this matrix before declaring the account "mapped." The Hunter agent should proactively surface contacts across these roles, not just the most senior person.

## PART 2: LEAD DISCOVERY ENGINE

### 2.1 Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) Framework

ARIA must help users define ICPs with scientific and operational precision. Generic criteria like "biotech companies" or "pharma firms" are too broad. ARIA should prompt for and track these ICP dimensions:

**Firmographic Filters:**
- Sub-sector (from taxonomy above)
- Revenue range / employee count
- Geography (HQ + operational footprint)
- Ownership structure (public, PE-backed, VC-funded, family-owned, academic spinout)
- Therapeutic area focus (oncology, immunology, CNS, cardiovascular, rare disease, cell & gene, etc.)
- Pipeline stage distribution (preclinical-heavy vs. commercial)
- Modality (small molecule, large molecule/biologics, cell therapy, gene therapy, RNA, ADC, etc.)

**Operational Filters (ARIA's differentiator):**
- Manufacturing footprint (in-house vs. outsourced)
- Clinical trial activity (number of active trials, phase distribution)
- Regulatory posture (FDA, EMA, PMDA, NMPA approvals/submissions)
- Technology stack signals (Veeva vs. Salesforce, SAP vs. Oracle, etc.)
- Outsourcing model (full-service CRO vs. FSP vs. in-house)
- Recent facility investments (expansions, new sites, GMP upgrades)

**Behavioral / Intent Filters:**
- Hiring patterns (what roles are they adding? commercial team build-out?)
- Conference presence (presenting? exhibiting? sponsoring?)
- Content consumption (topics they're engaging with)
- Vendor review activity (G2, Gartner, analyst briefings)
- Partnership announcements (new CRO/CDMO relationships)

**Financial Health Filters:**
- Funding runway (for pre-revenue biotech: months of cash remaining)
- Recent funding round (Series A/B/C/D, IPO, debt financing)
- Revenue growth trajectory
- M&A activity (acquiring or being acquired?)
- Patent cliff exposure (for pharma)

### 2.3 Lead Discovery Workflows

**Workflow 1: Territory-Based Discovery (Proactive)**
```
Trigger: User defines territory parameters (geography, sub-sector, size)
1. Hunter builds ICP from user inputs + corporate memory
2. Hunter queries Exa Company Search with ICP-matched criteria
3. For each qualifying company:
   a. Analyst enriches with ClinicalTrials.gov trial data
   b. Analyst enriches with FDA regulatory history
   c. Scout checks for recent trigger events (last 90 days)
   d. Hunter queries Exa People Search for buying committee contacts
4. Score each lead (see Section 3.2)
5. Present ranked list to user with justification per lead
6. Upon approval, store in discovered_leads + lead_memory
```

**Workflow 2: Signal-Triggered Discovery (Reactive)**
```
Trigger: Scout detects trigger event for tracked or untracked company
1. Scout classifies event type (see Section 2.4)
2. If company is already a lead:
   - Update lead record, adjust health score
   - Surface as Action Queue item if high-priority
3. If company is NOT a lead but matches ICP:
   - Hunter auto-creates lead record (lifecycle_stage: "signal_detected")
   - Analyst enriches company
   - Hunter finds contacts
   - Present to user: "ARIA detected [event] at [company]. They match your ICP. Should I add them to your pipeline?"
```

**Workflow 3: Lookalike Discovery**
```
Trigger: User asks "Find companies like [won account]"
1. Analyst builds profile of reference account: sub-sector, size, therapeutic area, modality, outsourcing model
2. Hunter queries Exa FindSimilar with reference company URL
3. Hunter queries Exa Company Search with extracted attributes
4. Filter results against existing pipeline (dedup)
5. Score and present
```

**Workflow 4: Event-Based Discovery**
```
Trigger: Major conference (JPM, BIO, ASCO, CPHI) approaching
1. Scout pulls exhibitor/speaker list from conference website
2. Hunter cross-references with ICP criteria
3. Analyst enriches matching companies with pipeline/financial data
4. Hunter finds attendee contacts
5. Present pre-conference target list: "These 15 companies at [conference] match your ICP. Here are the contacts and talking points for each."
```

## PART 3: LEAD QUALIFICATION & SCORING

### 3.1 Life Sciences Lead Scoring Model

ARIA should compute a composite lead score (0-100) from four weighted dimensions. This is not a generic BANT model; it's purpose-built for life sciences buying dynamics.

**Dimension 1: ICP Fit Score (30% weight)**

| Factor | Points | Logic |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Sub-sector match | 0-10 | Exact match to user's target sub-sector = 10 |
| Revenue/size match | 0-8 | Within ICP range = 8 |
| Geographic match | 0-5 | Territory alignment |
| Therapeutic area relevance | 0-7 | Matches user's domain expertise |

**Dimension 2: Trigger Signal Score (30% weight)**

| Factor | Points | Logic |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Category A trigger (pipeline/regulatory) | 0-15 | Active trigger in last 90 days = 15 |
| Category B trigger (financial/corporate) | 0-10 | Active trigger in last 90 days = 10 |
| Category C trigger (operational) | 0-10 | Active trigger in last 90 days = 10 |
| Multiple concurrent triggers | 0-5 bonus | 2+ triggers compound = +5 |
| Trigger recency | Multiplier | Last 30 days = 1.0x; 31-60 = 0.8x; 61-90 = 0.6x |

**Dimension 3: Engagement & Relationship Score (25% weight)**

| Factor | Points | Logic |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Existing relationship (user knows someone) | 0-10 | Direct relationship = 10; 2nd degree = 5 |
| Prior interaction history in ARIA | 0-8 | Meetings, emails, calls logged |
| Conference co-attendance | 0-4 | Overlap at recent events |
| Warm introduction available | 0-3 | Mutual connection identified |

**Dimension 4: Buying Readiness Score (15% weight)**

| Factor | Points | Logic |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Hiring for relevant roles | 0-5 | Actively hiring roles your solution supports |
| Technology evaluation signals | 0-5 | RFP issued, demo requests, analyst briefing |
| Budget cycle alignment | 0-3 | Within 3 months of budget planning period |
| Competitive displacement opportunity | 0-2 | Incumbent vendor issues detected |

**Score Interpretation:**
- 80-100: **Hot** -- Immediate outreach. Multiple buying signals, strong fit, relationship exists.
- 60-79: **Warm** -- Priority outreach within 2 weeks. Good fit with active triggers.
- 40-59: **Developing** -- Nurture track. Monitor for additional signals before outreach.
- 20-39: **Watch** -- Low urgency but meets ICP. Add to monitoring queue.
- 0-19: **Deprioritize** -- Poor fit or no signals. Review quarterly.

### 3.3 Disqualification Criteria

ARIA should also know when to deprioritize or disqualify a lead. These are life-sciences-specific disqualifiers:

- **Compliance ban on vendor engagement:** Some pharma companies impose temporary vendor freezes (especially during M&A integration)
- **Budget already committed:** Annual vendor selections completed; next evaluation window is 12+ months away
- **Wrong stage:** Pre-Series A biotech with no funding for commercial tools
- **Therapeutic area mismatch:** Company's pipeline has shifted to areas outside user's expertise
- **Debarment / consent decree:** Company under FDA consent decree may be too risk-averse for new vendors
- **Geographic restriction:** Operations entirely outside serviceable territory
- **Cultural/language barrier:** Decision-making happens in a market user cannot serve

## PART 5: OUTREACH INTELLIGENCE

### 5.1 The Life Sciences Outreach Philosophy

**Core principle:** In life sciences, every outreach message represents an opportunity to build or destroy scientific credibility. Surface-level personalization (adding a name and company) is immediately detected and dismissed by life sciences professionals. ARIA must demonstrate genuine understanding of the prospect's science, business context, and current challenges.

**What works in life sciences outreach:**
- Reference a specific, recent event at the prospect's company (trial readout, approval, acquisition)
- Demonstrate understanding of their therapeutic area or manufacturing challenge
- Lead with insight, not product pitch
- Keep messages short (life sciences professionals are time-compressed: lab schedules, trial milestones, global collaboration)
- Respect scientific expertise -- never talk down to a PhD
- Offer value first: relevant data, a comparable case study, a perspective on their competitive landscape

**What fails in life sciences outreach:**
- Generic "I noticed your company does [industry]" openers
- Marketing-heavy copy with exclamation points and buzzwords
- Requesting "15 minutes of your time" without offering clear value
- Ignoring the regulatory/compliance context
- Sending the same message to a scientist and a procurement officer
- Following up more than 3-4 times without adding new value

### 5.2 Persona-Specific Messaging Frameworks

**Persona: VP/SVP Commercial / CCO**
- Concerns: Revenue growth, market share, commercial execution, competitive positioning
- Language: Strategic, ROI-focused, competitive intelligence, market access
- Approach: Lead with competitive insight or market trend they may not have seen. Reference their recent earnings call language or conference presentation. Quantify potential impact.
- Tone: Peer-level, confident, concise

**Persona: VP/Director R&D / CSO**
- Concerns: Pipeline productivity, scientific rigor, publication record, innovation
- Language: Technical but not jargon-heavy, evidence-based, data-driven
- Approach: Reference a specific program in their pipeline. Cite relevant published data. Offer scientific value (application note, case study, technical comparison).
- Tone: Collegial, scientifically precise, respectful of expertise

**Persona: VP Procurement / Head of Sourcing**
- Concerns: Cost optimization, vendor qualification, compliance, contract terms
- Language: TCO, compliance, qualification, audit, SLA, vendor management
- Approach: Lead with operational efficiency or cost insight. Reference industry benchmark data. Emphasize compliance credentials.
- Tone: Professional, structured, detail-oriented

**Persona: VP Quality / Regulatory**
- Concerns: Compliance, audit readiness, data integrity, 21 CFR Part 11, GxP
- Language: Regulatory, validation, qualification, audit trail, deviation management
- Approach: Reference a recent regulatory development (new FDA guidance, enforcement action). Offer compliance-relevant content.
- Tone: Conservative, precise, risk-aware

**Persona: VP Manufacturing / Operations (CDMO)**
- Concerns: Capacity utilization, yield optimization, technology transfer, GMP compliance
- Language: Process development, scale-up, batch record, CAPA, OEE
- Approach: Reference their recent capacity expansion or new client announcement. Offer operational benchmarking data.
- Tone: Practical, operations-focused, results-oriented

### 5.3 Outreach Sequencing Strategy

Life sciences requires a thoughtful multi-touch approach. Pressure is counterproductive; persistence with added value is essential.

**Recommended Sequence (7-touch, 30-day cycle):**

| Touch | Day | Channel | Content |
|-------|-----|---------|---------|
| 1 | Day 1 | Email | Trigger-based insight + value offer (no ask) |
| 2 | Day 4 | LinkedIn | Connection request with personalized note referencing email topic |
| 3 | Day 8 | Email | Share relevant content (case study, market data) related to their trigger |
| 4 | Day 14 | Phone/voicemail | Brief, reference previous emails, offer specific value |
| 5 | Day 18 | Email | New angle or different stakeholder pain point |
| 6 | Day 24 | LinkedIn | Engage with their content or share relevant industry news |
| 7 | Day 30 | Email | Final value-add; offer to reconnect in future if timing is wrong |

**After 7 touches with no response:** Move to nurture. Monitor for new trigger events. Re-engage only when a meaningful new signal emerges.

**Timing considerations for life sciences:**
- Best days: Tuesday through Thursday
- Avoid: Monday mornings (clinical teams in review meetings), Friday afternoons
- Consider time zones carefully for global pharma
- Lab-based scientists check email early morning (6-8 AM) and late afternoon (4-6 PM)
- Conference weeks: don't email during the conference; follow up the week after
- JPM week (January): everyone is in meetings; wait until the following week
- ASCO/ASH/AACR: oncology professionals are unreachable during these conferences

### 5.4 Subject Line Intelligence

Life sciences email subject lines that perform:
- Reference a specific trigger: "Congratulations on the Phase III readout"
- Offer scientific insight: "New data on [therapeutic area] competitive landscape"
- Ask a relevant question: "How is [company] approaching [specific challenge]?"
- Keep it short: 4-8 words max
- Never use: "Quick question," "Following up," "Touching base," or any clickbait

## PART 6: LEAD NURTURING & PIPELINE MANAGEMENT

### 6.1 Nurture Track Design

Life sciences leads often require months of nurturing before they become opportunities. ARIA should manage these tracks autonomously.

**Track 1: Pipeline Milestone Nurture**
- For biotech/pharma leads with upcoming pipeline events
- ARIA monitors ClinicalTrials.gov and FDA calendars
- When milestone approaches, auto-triggers relevant outreach
- Example: "Their Phase III data readout is in 6 weeks. Time to re-engage about commercial launch readiness."

**Track 2: Budget Cycle Nurture**
- For leads that said "not now, check back next quarter/year"
- ARIA tracks their budget planning cycle
- Re-engages 2-3 months before planning period with fresh insight
- Example: "Q3 budget planning starts in 6 weeks at [pharma company]. Share the updated ROI analysis."

**Track 3: Relationship-Based Nurture**
- For leads where the user has a relationship but no active opportunity
- ARIA shares relevant industry content, congratulates on milestones, monitors news
- Maintains warm relationship without pushing
- Example: "Rob's contact at Cytiva just published a paper. Send congratulations + relevant market data."

**Track 4: Competitive Displacement Nurture**
- For leads using a competitor's solution
- ARIA monitors for competitor issues (outage, recall, price increase, bad press)
- Triggers outreach when displacement opportunity emerges
- Example: "Competitor X had a data breach reported yesterday. Three of your leads use their platform."

### 6.2 Pipeline Health Monitoring

ARIA should continuously monitor pipeline health with these life-sciences-specific indicators:

**Healthy pipeline indicators:**
- Leads distributed across lifecycle stages (not all in same stage)
- Multiple stakeholders engaged at opportunity stage
- Recent activity (email, meeting, call) within last 2 weeks for warm+ leads
- Active trigger events feeding new leads monthly
- Pipeline value at 3x+ quota coverage

**Warning signals ARIA should surface:**
- Lead gone dark for 30+ days without response
- Single-threaded opportunity (only one contact engaged)
- No trigger event supporting timing (why would they buy now?)
- Procurement-led evaluation without champion (price pressure, low win rate)
- Stalled opportunity with no next step scheduled
- Pipeline concentrated in one sub-sector or one deal size

### 6.3 Win/Loss Analysis Intelligence

After every closed deal (won or lost), ARIA should capture:
- Key factors in decision (technical, commercial, relationship)
- Competitive situation (who else was evaluated)
- Timeline from first touch to close
- Which trigger event originated the opportunity
- Which stakeholders were most influential
- What content/collateral was most impactful
- Pricing dynamics (were they price-sensitive? Value-driven?)

This data feeds back into ARIA's scoring models, making future lead gen increasingly precise.

## PART 7: LIFE SCIENCES DATA INTELLIGENCE

### 7.1 Scientific Database Intelligence

ARIA should mine scientific databases not just for research, but for commercial intelligence. This is a key differentiator.

**ClinicalTrials.gov Commercial Intelligence:**
- Track trial registrations by company to detect pipeline expansion
- Monitor trial status changes (recruiting, completed, terminated) for timing signals
- Identify which CROs/CDMOs are listed as collaborators
- Map principal investigator networks for KOL intelligence
- Detect geographic expansion (new trial sites = new market entry)
- Count active trials by therapeutic area to gauge investment priority

**PubMed Commercial Intelligence:**
- Publication frequency by company/author = innovation activity level
- Collaboration networks from co-author analysis
- Emerging therapeutic areas from trending MeSH terms
- KOL identification from publication volume and citation counts

**FDA Intelligence:**
- PDUFA calendar for outreach timing
- 510(k) database for medtech launch signals
- Warning letters/483s for quality-driven buying
- Orange Book for patent expiry intelligence
- Approved drug labeling changes for market access signals

### 7.2 Financial Intelligence for Life Sciences

**SEC Filing Intelligence:**
- 10-K risk factors: Reveals strategic concerns and investment priorities
- 10-Q revenue breakdown: Shows which business units are growing
- 8-K current reports: M&A, leadership changes, material events
- Proxy statements: Executive compensation tied to commercial metrics
- Earnings transcripts keyword tracking: "invest," "consolidate," "partner," "outsource," "efficiency," "digital," "AI"

**Funding Intelligence for Biotech:**
- Series B+: Company has validation, needs to build commercial infrastructure
- Series C/D: Commercial launch approaching, full stack needed
- IPO: Maximum spending on infrastructure, compliance, and visibility
- Crossover round: Strong institutional validation, 12-18 months to IPO

### 7.3 Competitive Intelligence for Lead Gen

ARIA should maintain competitive intelligence not just on the user's direct competitors, but on the target company's competitive landscape, because understanding their competitive pressures reveals buying triggers.

**For each target account, ARIA should track:**
- Who are their top 3 competitors?
- Has a competitor recently launched, been acquired, or had a setback?
- Is market share shifting? (earnings call language is the best signal)
- Are they under pricing pressure from biosimilars/generics?
- Is a new entrant disrupting their market?

This competitive context makes ARIA's outreach dramatically more relevant: "Given [competitor's] recent market entry in your [therapeutic area], how are you thinking about your commercial strategy?"

## PART 8: COMPLIANCE & ETHICS FRAMEWORK

### 8.1 Regulatory Compliance in Outreach

ARIA must ensure all lead generation activity complies with applicable regulations:

**CAN-SPAM / GDPR / CCPA:**
- Honor unsubscribe requests immediately
- Include physical address in email campaigns
- Maintain opt-out lists per regulation
- GDPR: Legitimate interest basis for B2B outreach, but must stop on request
- Track consent status per contact per jurisdiction

**Life Sciences Specific:**
- Never make product claims that haven't been through MLR review
- Don't reference specific patient outcomes without proper context
- Avoid discussing off-label uses in outreach
- Be cautious with pricing claims (Anti-Kickback Statute considerations)
- Sunshine Act: Track any transfer of value to HCPs

**ARIA's approach:** Flag potential compliance issues in draft outreach before sending. When in doubt, recommend the user consult their legal/compliance team.

### 8.2 Data Ethics

- Never scrape contact data from sources that prohibit it
- Respect LinkedIn's terms of service
- Don't use personal health information in outreach
- Don't reference insider knowledge of competitor pricing
- Always disclose AI involvement in communications when required by company policy

## APPENDIX C: ANTI-PATTERNS -- WHAT ARIA MUST NEVER DO

1. **Never send identical messages to multiple contacts at the same company.** Life sciences buying committees talk to each other. Duplicate outreach kills credibility.
2. **Never reference a clinical trial failure without sensitivity.** A failed trial can mean job losses and patient impact. Frame competitively, never gleefully.
3. **Never guess at scientific terminology.** If ARIA is unsure about a modality, mechanism, or regulatory term, look it up first.
4. **Never ignore the compliance/quality stakeholder.** In regulated environments, they can veto any purchase.
5. **Never treat a CDMO the same as a pharma company.** Different business model, different buyers, different timelines.
6. **Never spam during conference weeks.** Everyone is in meetings. Wait until the week after.
7. **Never use "hope this email finds you well" or "just checking in."** Life sciences professionals see through empty filler.
8. **Never promise regulatory outcomes.** ARIA can share data but cannot claim FDA approval likelihood.
9. **Never share one company's proprietary intelligence with another.** Even aggregated "benchmarking" must be properly anonymized.
10. **Never batch leads by company size alone.** A 50-person biotech with a Phase III asset is a better lead than a 5,000-person diagnostics company with flat growth.
